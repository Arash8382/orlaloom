## How high should café curtains hang?

The rule that makes café curtains look deliberate rather than accidental: **mount the rod at the exact midpoint of the window, or just above it.** You want the top of the panel to hit roughly halfway up the glass, so the tier covers the bottom for privacy while the top half stays open and bright. Sitting it too low looks skimpy; too high and you lose the whole point of the open-topped look.

A few specifics that help:

- **Mount inside the frame** where you can, using a slim rod (a spring-tension rod like the KXLife is perfect and needs no drilling). An inside mount keeps the clean, tucked-in café look.
- **Add a second rod at the very top** if you want the classic tiered effect — a valance or a second short panel up high, tiers down low. That's where a checkerboard valance over a plain gingham tier works beautifully.
- **Let the hem land at or just below the sill,** not pooling on the counter. Café tiers are meant to be short and tidy — this is not the place for puddled floor-length drapes.
- **Measure width for gather.** For soft folds rather than a flat, stretched panel, you want total curtain width around 1.5 to 2 times your window width.

## Gingham vs. checkerboard vs. buffalo check

These three names get used interchangeably, but they're genuinely different looks, and picking the right one is most of the decision:

- **Gingham** is the fine, small check — usually a colour woven against white, in squares under about half an inch. It's the softest and most traditionally cottage of the three (think the VOGOL blue or the ruffled Vermont tiers). Yarn-dyed gingham, where the pattern is woven in rather than printed, looks best and lasts longest.
- **Buffalo check** is the same idea scaled up — big, bold squares, usually two saturated colours like red-and-white or black-and-white. It reads more farmhouse and graphic (the Barossa and Haperlare pairs). Great in a kitchen that needs a strong, simple pattern.
- **Checkerboard** is the current trend twist: an even, high-contrast grid of solid squares — more chessboard than fabric-check, and often printed rather than woven. It's the boldest, most modern of the three and works in small doses, like the lovemyfabric valance up top.

## FAQ

**Are café curtains enough privacy for a kitchen?**

For most kitchens, yes — they cover the bottom half of the window at exactly counter and sink height, which is where privacy actually matters. You keep the light and the view of the sky up top while blocking sightlines below. If you need full blackout or total privacy at night, pair a tier with a plain roller blind behind it rather than relying on the café panel alone.

**Do gingham café curtains work in a small kitchen?**

They're ideal for one. Small kitchens are mostly hard surfaces, so a single soft, patterned tier is often the only textile in the room and carries the whole cozy budget. Keep the check small-to-medium scale in a tight space (a fine gingham or the sage buffalo rather than a giant checkerboard) so the pattern doesn't overwhelm the window.

**Can I machine-wash these?**

Almost all of the Amazon tiers here are machine washable — cold water, gentle cycle, and hang or lay flat to dry. Skip the tumble dryer, which is what warps and shrinks yarn-dyed cotton blends. A quick steam or press while slightly damp gets the folds looking crisp again.

**What size café curtains do I need?**

Measure your window width and buy total curtain width of about 1.5 to 2 times that number for proper gather (two panels usually does it). For length, measure from where you'll mount the rod — the window midpoint — down to the sill; most café tiers run 24 to 36 inches, so pick the drop closest to that measurement. **Gingham or checkerboard — which is more timeless?**

Gingham. Small-check gingham and buffalo check have been in cottage kitchens for generations and aren't going anywhere. Checkerboard is having a bright, fun moment right now but is more of a trend, so it's best used in a small, easily-swapped dose like a valance — while a classic gingham tier is something you can happily leave up for years.
