**The short answer: in a living room, choose a rug big enough that at least the front legs of every sofa and chair sit on it — usually an 8x10. In a bedroom, a 5x7 beside or partly under the bed does the job. Kitchens and hallways want a 2x6 to 2.5x10 runner, and reading corners and small round tables love a 4 to 6 ft round.** Nearly every rug that looks "off" in a room is simply too small, so when you are torn between two sizes, take the bigger one.

That is the whole guide in a paragraph — but the details are where rooms go from fine to storybook. Here is how to size a rug for every room in a cottagecore home, with washable, floral examples in each size so you can see what the numbers mean in practice.

## The one rule that fixes most rooms

Interior designers repeat it because it works: **a rug should connect the furniture, not float between pieces.** When a rug touches every seat in a conversation area — even just the front legs — the eye reads the whole arrangement as one cosy island. When a small rug floats in the middle with furniture pushed to the walls, the room looks smaller and the rug looks like a bath mat that wandered.

The cottagecore aesthetic amplifies this. Faded florals and scalloped edges read as "collected over generations" only when the rug feels generous; a tiny floral rug in a big room reads as an afterthought. Size first, pattern second.

## What size rug for a living room?

**Default to 8x10.** In a typical 12 x 15 ft living room, an 8x10 lets the sofa's front legs and both armchairs land on the rug with walkway to spare. Go 9x12 in an open-plan space where the "living room" is really a zone you are drawing inside a bigger room — the rug is what defines the zone. A 5x7 only suits a genuinely small sitting room, a snug, or a rug layered over carpet.

Leave 12 to 18 inches of bare floor between the rug's edge and the walls so the floor frames the rug like a mat frames a print. ## What size rug for a bedroom?

You have two good options. The classic is a big rug **under the bed**: an 8x10 under a queen, placed so it extends about 2 ft beyond each side — bare feet land on warm floral, not cold floor. The budget-and-laundry-friendly cottage option is a **5x7 beside or across the foot of the bed**, which gives you most of the underfoot comfort for half the price and actually fits in a home washing machine — no small thing if the rug is pale.

Runners also moonlight beautifully in bedrooms: a 2x6 down the side of the bed is the smallest-commitment version of the same idea. Our [floral bedroom rugs guide](/blog/floral-bedroom-rugs) ranks the softest picks for exactly this room.

## What size rug for a dining room?

One rule decides it: **chairs must stay on the rug when pulled out.** Measure your table and add roughly 24 to 30 inches on every side. In practice that means an 8x10 for most 6-seater tables and a 9x12 for anything longer. If the numbers say your room cannot fit that, skip the rug — chair legs catching a rug edge twice a meal is worse than bare floor.

Round tables are the exception where a **round rug** shines: a pedestal breakfast table over the Nina's 6 ft round is a genuinely lovely cottage corner. For a full-size dining table with chairs, though, stick to the rectangles.

## Kitchens, hallways and entries: runner sizes

Runners are measured by lane, not room. A **2x6** suits the strip in front of a single sink — the Solace yellow above exists for exactly that spot. A **2.5x8 or 2.5x10** runs a galley kitchen or hallway; leave about 6 inches of floor showing on each side and a foot at each end. For entries, a 2x3 or 3x5 with low pile handles wet boots best. We tested six kitchen-specific options in the [washable kitchen runner guide](/blog/washable-kitchen-runner-rugs).

## Where round rugs earn their keep

Rounds are the most under-used size in the house. A **4 ft round** transforms leftover corners — under a reading chair, in front of a dresser, beside a bathtub, under a nursery rocker. A **6 ft round** anchors a small pedestal table or softens a square landing. Because they are small, rounds are also where you can risk the sweetest prints — roses, scallops, borders — without committing a whole room to them.

## Two mistakes to avoid

**Buying to the furniture you have pushed against the walls.** Pull seating toward the centre first, then measure — most rooms want furniture off the walls anyway, and the rug size follows from the arrangement, not the skirting boards.

**Skipping the pad.** Thin washable rugs are wonderful and they are also thin. A felt-and-rubber pad one size down from the rug stops the skating, adds the cushion, and makes a fifty-dollar rug feel like a two-hundred-dollar one. It is the cheapest upgrade in this guide.

## FAQ

**Is a 5x7 rug big enough for a living room?**
Only for a small one. In a standard living room a 5x7 floats awkwardly between the furniture; an 8x10 that reaches the front legs of every seat almost always looks better. Save the 5x7 for bedrooms, snugs and layering.

**What size rug goes under a queen bed?**
An 8x10 placed under the bed, extending about 2 ft on each side, is the classic answer. A 5x7 across the lower two-thirds of the bed is the budget version, and a 2x6 runner along each side is the washable-friendly version.

**Should a rug be bigger or smaller than the room?**
Always smaller, with 12 to 18 inches of visible floor around the edges in main rooms. Wall-to-wall rug reads as carpet; a border of floor makes the rug — and the room — look intentional.

**Do washable rugs come in big sizes like 9x12?**
Yes — several of the picks here go to 9x12 and the Giverny to 10x14 — but remember that anything over about 6x9 will not fit a home washing machine and needs a laundromat front-loader on wash day.
